# Soap Formulation: Expert Intuition Guide

## The Core Reaction

Saponification is alkaline hydrolysis of fats. A triglyceride (glycerol backbone + 3 fatty acid chains) meets a strong base → the hydroxide ions cleave the ester bonds → you get 3 fatty acid salt molecules (soap) + free glycerol. The reaction is exothermic. Everything else in soap making is controlling *which* fatty acid salts you end up with and *how* they arrange themselves.

## NaOH vs KOH: It's About the Ion Size

- **Sodium (NaOH)** → small ion → tight crystalline packing → hard bar soap. Less soluble in water, so it dissolves slowly in use (lasts longer).
- **Potassium (KOH)** → larger ion → disordered, hydrated structure → soft paste or liquid soap. Much more soluble, dissolves instantly.
- **Mix both** for in-between textures. Shaving soaps do this: firm enough to hold shape in a puck, soluble enough to load onto a brush easily.

Mental model: sodium soap is a brick wall, potassium soap is a pile of sand. Mixing gives you adobe.

## Cold Process vs Hot Process: Patience vs Speed

**Cold Process (CP):** Mix oils + lye at ~30–50°C. Pour into molds at "trace" (pudding consistency). Saponification completes in ~24hrs in the mold, but needs 4–6 weeks cure for water to evaporate. You get smooth, designable bars. Delicate additives survive better. Downside: long wait, and the still-active lye can destroy botanicals and color.

**Hot Process (HP):** Cook the batter (crockpot, oven) for 1–2 hours until saponification is complete. Soap becomes thick mashed-potato texture. Scoop into molds. Usable almost immediately (maybe 1–2 weeks drying). Additives (fragrance, superfat oils) can be stirred in *after* cook, so they survive intact. Downside: rustic, lumpy appearance — no fancy swirls.

Think of CP as "let the reaction happen at its own pace" and HP as "refluxing a reaction to completion."

## Fatty Acids: The Personality of Your Soap

This is the core of formulation. Every oil is a mix of fatty acids, and each fatty acid gives your soap specific traits.

### Saturated Fatty Acids (straight chains, pack tightly)

| Fatty Acid | Chain | What It Does | Watch Out For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Lauric** (C12) | Short | Big fluffy bubbles, strong cleansing, hard bar | Very drying — strips skin oils aggressively. Bar dissolves fast despite being hard when dry. |
| **Myristic** (C14) | Short | Similar to lauric — bubbly, cleansing | Same drying concerns. |
| **Palmitic** (C16) | Long | Hard bar, dense/creamy small-bubble lather, long-lasting | Too much (>50%) = waxy bar that barely lathers. |
| **Stearic** (C18) | Long | Very hard bar, stable cream lather, slow to dissolve | Same over-hardness risk as palmitic. |

**Key insight:** Short-chain saturates (lauric/myristic) = instant lather + aggressive cleansing. Long-chain saturates (palmitic/stearic) = durability + cream lather. You almost always want both.

### Unsaturated Fatty Acids (kinked chains, resist packing)

| Fatty Acid | Chain | What It Does | Watch Out For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Oleic** (C18:1) | Mono-unsat | Gentle, conditioning, "lotion-like" slippery lather | Slow to harden, slimy lather on its own. Needs months of cure at high %. |
| **Linoleic** (C18:2) | Poly-unsat | Silky feel, conditioning | Prone to rancidity (DOS — Dreaded Orange Spots). Keep below ~15% total. |
| **Linolenic** (C18:3) | Poly-unsat | Similar to linoleic but worse rancidity risk | Even more oxidation-prone. Use sparingly. |
| **Ricinoleic** (C18:1 OH) | Hydroxy-unsat | Lather stabilizer — keeps foam from collapsing, adds slip/creaminess | Accelerates trace despite being unsaturated. Cap at ~5–10%. |

**Key insight:** Unsaturates = conditioning and mildness. But they make bars softer and polyunsaturates go rancid. Ricinoleic (castor oil) is the exception — it's a lather *multiplier* that makes everything else work better.

### The Formulation Balancing Act

A well-rounded bar soap typically aims for roughly:
- **~15% lauric + myristic** → fluffy cleansing lather
- **~30% palmitic + stearic** → hardness + creamy stable lather
- **Remainder oleic** → conditioning, mildness
- **~5% ricinoleic (castor)** → lather boost and stability
- **Keep linoleic + linolenic < 15%** → avoid rancidity

## Trace Speed Rules of Thumb

- High saturated fat % → fast trace (batter thickens quickly)
- High unsaturated fat % → slow trace (long working time)
- 100% olive oil = extremely slow trace
- Castor oil accelerates trace even though it's unsaturated (the hydroxyl group)
- Certain fragrance oils (floral, spicy) can accelerate trace or cause seizing

## Additives Cheat Sheet

### Hardness & Structure
- **Salt (NaCl)** at 1–3% of oil weight: hardens bar, helps mold release. Too much = grainy separation.
- **Sodium lactate** at 1–3% of oil weight: hardens bar, smoother unmolding, makes HP soap more pourable. Above 5% = brittle or sweaty bars.

### Lather Boosters
- **Sugar/honey/milk** (contain sugars): boost bubble volume and stabilize foam. But sugars fuel the exotherm — expect overheating. Milk soaps scorch easily. Keep honey to 1–3% of oils, soap cool.

### Preservation & Longevity
- **Chelators** (sodium citrate, EDTA, sodium gluconate): bind metal ions → prevent soap scum in hard water AND slow rancidity by neutralizing pro-oxidant metals. Use at 0.1–1%. Citric acid in lye water converts to sodium citrate in situ (add extra lye to compensate for the neutralization).
- **Antioxidants** (vitamin E / tocopherols, rosemary oleoresin extract): scavenge free radicals in the unsaponified oils, slowing DOS. Add at trace, ~0.5% of oils. Limited effectiveness in high pH.

### Color
- **Micas, iron oxides, ultramarines**: stable in high pH — reliable color.
- **Plant-based pigments**: usually destroyed. Anthocyanins turn brown/green. Chlorophyll goes olive-drab. Calendula petals are a rare exception (stay yellow).
- **Clays**: muted earth tones + silkier lather + can anchor fragrance. 1–3% of oil weight.
- **Vanillin** (in vanilla/bakery fragrances): will oxidize to brown in alkaline soap. Titanium dioxide can lighten but won't fully counteract heavy vanillin.

### Fragrance
- Spicy/floral EOs (eugenol-heavy — cinnamon, clove) tend to **accelerate trace or seize**.
- Citrus EOs **fade fast** — volatile terpenes evaporate during saponification.
- Heavy/woody scents (patchouli, cedarwood) **stick well**.
- In HP, add fragrance *after* cook to preserve delicate notes.

### Botanicals & Exfoliants
- Most flower petals → turn brown/black in lye. Use only on top as decoration post-pour.
- Calendula petals → one of the few that survive with color intact.
- Oatmeal, poppy seeds, pumice → chemically inert, just texture.
- Coffee grounds → may leach color.

## Superfat (Lye Discount)

Intentionally leaving 5–8% of oils unsaponified. This free oil stays dispersed in the soap and deposits a thin conditioning film on skin during use. 

- **5%** = standard gentle soap. Good balance of conditioning vs shelf life.
- **15–20%+** = feels greasy, goes rancid faster (especially if the leftover oils are polyunsaturated).
- In CP, you can't control *which* oils remain unsaponified (lye doesn't discriminate). In HP, you can add a specific luxury oil after cook to guarantee it stays as superfat.
- Superfat is essentially free oil in an alkaline matrix — it's the main rancidity vector.

## Glycerin Dynamics

Glycerin is a natural byproduct — it stays in handmade soap (unlike commercial soap, which strips it out). It's a humectant (draws moisture), which is great for skin feel but makes soap softer and can cause "glycerin dew" (soap sweats in humid conditions). Industrial soap "salts out" the soap from glycerin by adding NaCl — the sodium soap precipitates, glycerin stays in solution.

## Common Formulation Archetypes

**Hard luxury bar:** High palmitic/stearic (tallow, palm, cocoa butter) + moderate coconut + olive. NaOH. Add sodium lactate. CP for smooth finish. Dense, long-lasting, creamy lather.

**High-lather cleanser:** Heavy coconut/palm kernel (lauric/myristic). NaOH. High superfat (15–20%) to offset stripping. Add sugar for extra bubbles. Instant big lather, squeaky clean feel.

**Gentle face/baby soap:** Mostly olive + avocado, some shea butter. Low lauric/myristic. 5–8% superfat. Add colloidal oatmeal, sodium citrate. Minimal lather but very mild — oleate soap has lower micelle-forming ability, cleans less aggressively.

**Liquid soap:** KOH + mostly olive or sunflower, touch of coconut/castor. HP (cook to paste, then dilute with water). Glycerin stays in, keeps it clear. Small amount of NaCl can thicken (converts some K-soap to Na-soap, reducing solubility = thicker).

**Shaving soap:** Mix NaOH + KOH. Very high stearic (>50%). Firm puck that loads easily. Dense, stable, cushioning lather. Castor for slip and foam stability. Glycerin for glide.

## Expert Mental Models

1. **Soap is not one substance** — it's a microscopic network of crystalline soap regions, amorphous soap/glycerin zones, water, and dispersed additives. Hardness comes from how well soap molecules stack. Saturated + sodium = tight stacking. Unsaturated + potassium = loose stacking.

2. **Solubility is the master variable.** It controls how fast the bar dissolves (longevity), how quickly lather forms (ease of use), and how much oil the soap strips from skin (gentleness). Short-chain + potassium = most soluble. Long-chain + sodium = least soluble.

3. **Every formulation is a tension between cleansing and conditioning.** Lauric/myristic push toward cleansing. Oleic/linoleic push toward conditioning. Palmitic/stearic are neutral — they add structure without strongly pushing either direction.

4. **Temperature is a hidden ingredient.** It controls trace speed, gel phase, additive survival, and crystal formation. CP's slow cooling lets crystals arrange over weeks. HP forces fast solidification. Both get to the same chemistry, but the microstructure differs.

5. **The lye doesn't care about your intentions.** It will react with whatever oil it contacts — you can't selectively saponify one oil and leave another as superfat in CP. Only HP post-cook addition gives you true control over which oil remains free.
