# Superfat Selection: Expert Reference

## Core Mental Model

Bar soap = ionic surfactant crystal matrix + water + glycerin + **a dispersed free lipid phase (the superfat).**

In hot process, post-cook superfat is the one variable where you control exactly what remains as non-soap oil. It's a deliberate design layer.

## Structure Predicts Performance

Molecular architecture — wax ester vs. triglyceride, saturated vs. PUFA, unsaponifiable fraction — is more predictive than any single fatty acid number. In the right materials, stability, sensory impact, and lather compatibility all point the same direction. These properties cluster because they flow from the same structural features:

- **Low PUFA** → fewer oxidation sites → shelf stability
- **High unsaponifiables / waxy fraction** → high sensory impact per % → less total lipid needed → less foam penalty
- **Structured / less "free liquid triglyceride"** → resists micelle capture → deposits on skin rather than rinsing away

The formulation instinct: **find materials where these axes reinforce each other.**

### The Mechanism Stack

A superfat is excellent when three mechanisms reinforce:

1. **Deposits and persists at low dose.** Structured/waxy/high-unsaponifiable materials resist being pulled into micelles → they leave a thin film with less total lipid mass.

2. **High sensory impact per %.** Unsaponifiables (sterols, wax alcohols/esters, triterpenes) change glide/drag/afterfeel without requiring triglyceride volume.

3. **Oxidatively boring.** Low PUFA means the lipid phase is stable — no trade of "nice feel now" for "DOS later."

The same materials often satisfy all three. This is why jojoba and shea are reliably excellent — they stack wins rather than forcing compromises.

## Superfat Classes by Molecular Architecture

### Class 1 — Wax Esters & High-Unsaponifiable Materials
Not triglycerides. Behave differently in the soap matrix. **Use at 1–4% post-cook.**

| Material | Why It Stacks |
|---|---|
| **Jojoba** | Wax ester system. Very high unsaponifiable fraction. Dry/silky feel, not greasy. Highest impact per % of common superfats. Resists micelle solubilization. |
| **Lanolin** | Complex wax ester + sterol mixture. Biomimetic — close to human skin lipids. Excellent film persistence, minimal foam disruption at low %. |

### Class 2 — Sterol-Rich Butters
Solid-at-body-temp triglycerides with meaningful unsaponifiable fractions. Form structured lipid domains in the matrix. **Use at 2–5% post-cook.**

| Material | Profile |
|---|---|
| **Shea butter** | ~8–11% unsaponifiables + stearic/oleic backbone. Creamier lather and feel. Stacks film persistence (stearic) with emollient slip (oleic). |
| **Cocoa butter** | More structuring, increases perceived firmness/occlusion. Very stable saturated backbone. |
| **Mango butter** | Softer than cocoa. Middle-ground between structuring and slip. |

### Class 3 — Plain Triglycerides (Mono/Saturated)
Low unsaponifiables. Sensory impact comes from volume, not molecular leverage. Reliable and stable. **Use at 2–6%.**

| Material | Profile |
|---|---|
| **High-oleic sunflower/safflower** | Mostly monounsaturated. Decent stability, good mildness. |
| **Tallow / Lard** | Very stable palmitic/stearic/oleic profile. |
| **Olive oil** | High oleic, low PUFA. Good slip but no structural leverage — needs volume to register. |

### Class 4 — PUFA-Heavy Triglycerides
Multiple double bonds create oxidation-prone sites. The conditioning they provide is available from Class 2–3 without the risk. **Use at ≤1–2% only**, with aggressive chelation + antioxidant + storage discipline.

| Material | Risk |
|---|---|
| **Hemp, grapeseed, rosehip** | High linoleic/linolenic → fast peroxide formation → DOS. |
| **Evening primrose, borage** | Same oxidation profile, higher price. |

## The Base Matrix as Collaborator

The base soap determines how easy it is for a small superfat to do a lot of work.

**High lauric/myristic (coconut, palm kernel):** Micelles aggressively solubilize oils during washing — including your superfat. You need superfat that *resists* solubilization (Class 1–2), not just more oil. Moderate superfat (5–8%) with high-leverage picks.

**High stearic/palmitic (tallow, palm, cocoa butter):** Dense crystalline scaffold holds lipid droplets stably, solubilizes less aggressively. The base collaborates — small amounts feel big. Low–moderate superfat (1–4%).

**High oleic (olive, avocado):** Mild but foam is fragile. Adding liquid oil collapses what structure exists. Best with tiny amounts of Class 1–2 superfat (1–3%) for maximum leverage.

**Meaningful PUFA in base:** Oxidation risk is already elevated in the saponified portion. Superfat must be very stable. Chelation + antioxidants are non-optional. Stability engineering, not luxury oil selection.

### How Superfat Partitions During Use

During washing, superfat either stays as a lipid droplet in the matrix, gets solubilized into micelles and rinses off, or deposits a microfilm on skin.

- Laurate/myristate soaps emulsify aggressively → more superfat carried away. High-structure/waxy superfats resist this.
- Stearate/palmitate soaps solubilize gently → more reliable conditioned feel at lower %.
- Lighter, fluid triglycerides are easier to emulsify away. Waxy/structuring lipids stick around.

### Oxidation Is a System Property

DOS has two layers, not one:

**Layer A — The superfat pool:** PUFA oxidizes fast. Mono slower. Saturated/waxes slowest.

**Layer B — The soap matrix:** Trace metals + oxygen + light drive radical chemistry. PUFA in the base (even saponified) can seed oxidation that degrades the whole bar.

| Base + Superfat | Outcome |
|---|---|
| High-PUFA base + PUFA superfat | DOS speedrun |
| High-PUFA base + stable superfat + chelation | Manageable |
| Low-PUFA base + stable superfat | Set it and forget it |

## Superfat % Guidelines (Post-Cook HP)

| Range | Outcome |
|---|---|
| **1–3%** | Noticeable softening, minimal lather hit, low spoilage risk. **Best general zone.** |
| **4–7%** | Stronger emollient feel, mild lather reduction, higher DOS sensitivity. |
| **8–12%** | Deliberately oily/mild bar. Foam suppression is real. Stability must be engineered. |
| **15–20%** | Niche — taming very cleansing bases. Big compromises on lather, firmness, longevity. |

## Genuine Constraints

Not everything stacks. These are irreducible physics:

1. **Superfat % ceiling.** Beyond a point, any lipid phase damps foam — surfactant film loading is physics, not chemistry you can engineer around.
2. **PUFA ceiling.** PUFA-heavy superfat at high % will oxidize regardless. Chelation slows it; nothing eliminates it.
3. **Cost.** The most synergistic materials are expensive — use them at low % for leverage, not high % for volume.

## Stabilization Control Knobs

Superfat choice is half the system. Oxidation control is the other half.

- **Chelators** (EDTA, sodium citrate, sodium gluconate): Bind trace metals that catalyze oxidation. Fewer DOS events, better hard-water performance.
- **Antioxidants** (ROE, mixed tocopherols): Slow radical chain reactions in the lipid phase. Add post-cook.
- **Storage**: Cool, dark, low airflow. Wrap once cured.

## Ground Truth

What superfat actually does in a rinse-off product:

- **Real:** Reduces perceived stripping (thin lipid film + reduced effective detergent action). Sensory tuning and mildness.
- **Marginal:** Bioactive delivery — high pH, short contact time, rinse-off. Most actives don't survive or absorb meaningfully.
- **Marketing:** Label story and ingredient appeal.

The most rational superfat choice optimizes sensory experience and stability.

## Expert Mental Models

1. **Structure predicts performance.** Molecular architecture is more predictive than any single fatty acid percentage. When the structure is right, stability + feel + foam all reinforce.

2. **Unsaponifiables are the leverage point.** They deliver sensory impact without proportional foam suppression or lipid loading.

3. **Superfat is a sensory layer engineered into a surfactant matrix.** Great superfats minimize foam disruption as a consequence of their structure, not as a tax you're constantly paying.

4. **The base determines how easy the superfat's job is.** A stearic-rich base carries superfat elegantly. A high-lauric base fights the superfat for micelle space — you need materials that resist solubilization, not just more oil.

5. **Oxidation is a system property.** A stable superfat in a PUFA-rich base with no chelation will still go rancid. Think whole chain: metals → radicals → peroxides → off-notes.

6. **More superfat doesn't scale linearly.** 1–3% is where benefit per increment is highest. Past ~8%, you're mostly adding problems with diminishing returns.
